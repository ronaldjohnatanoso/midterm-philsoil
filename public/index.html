Philippine
Soil

Philippine soil is a diverse and complex subject due to the country's varied geography, climate, and geological history. The Philippines, an archipelago of over 7,000 islands in Southeast Asia, exhibits a wide range of soil types, each with its own unique characteristics.

The Philippines has a wide range of soil types due to its diverse geography and climate. There are approximately 9 major soil orders identified in the Philippines, which can be further classified into various suborders and soil series.

orders of.
Soil

The USDA Soil Taxonomic Classification is used in naming Philippine soils. The National Bureau of Soil and Water Management has identified nine Soil Orders in the country, namely: Inceptisols (39.9%), Ultisols (26.6%), Alfisols (17.1%), Entisols (8.1%), Vertisols (4.5%), Mollisols (3.3%), Andisols (0.23%), Oxisols (0.2%) and some limited Histosols

Land Classification.
land use

Out of the 30 million hectare land area of the Philippines, 52.9 percent or 15.9 million hectares are classified as forested land or public land. The remaining 47.1 percent, or 14.1 million hectares, is classified as alienable and disposable land, which is open to use by private individuals for agriculture and other purposes.

Impact of.
Soil

Soil is the foundation of agriculture, and the Philippines relies heavily on this sector for food production and livelihoods. The country's diverse soil types and climatic conditions influence crop choices and productivity. Fertile soils support the growth of a wide range of crops, including rice, corn, sugarcane, and various fruits.

Significance of Philippine Soil
Agricultural Planning

Farmers and agricultural businesses can use the soil type information to make informed decisions about crop selection, irrigation, and fertilization to maximize yields and reduce environmental impacts.

Land Use Planning

Urban planners and local government authorities can use soil data to make informed decisions about land use zoning, ensuring that areas with soil types suitable for constuction are utilized efficiently.

Disaster Risk Reduction

Certain soil types are more prone to natural disasters such as landslides and mudslides. Understanding the soil composition can contribute to better disaster risk assessment and preparedness. This is particularly important in a country like the Philippines, which is prone to various natural disasters.
